Executive summary

In expanding to the 25th floor, a new IDF had to be created that included provisioning for AV equipment. The AV equipment requires multicast flooding, and because VLAN tags were inconsistent in previous setup, the traffic was not appropriately segregated leading to a multicast flood that disabled the office networking.

Background

Multicast equipment is used by our AV network, and has a separate routing plane from the core network.

Incident Description

For roughly 30 minutes on December 1st, NYC experienced a line rate multicast flood from the core. This occurred after the NYC25 AGG switch was connected to the NYC25 AV switch.

Corrective Action

The cable connecting the NYC25 aggregation layer to the NYC25 AV stack was disconnected.

The following changes were made to the switch configuration:

Tags were unified across both sides of the lag, removing problematic VLANS

Analysis

The NYC AGG to AV links on other floors (21-24) include VLANs on the AGG side, that are not present on the AV side. During configuration of the NYC25 AV stack, I missed the lack of tags on the AV side, and created a loop.

Detection

How quickly was the problem detected?

It took roughly 30 minutes to be detected, due to the "ramping" nature of floods caused by loops.

How was the problem detected?

Via monitoring, and onsite wireless.

How can detection be improved?

Detection is fine here.

Response

How quickly did we respond once detected?

Instantly, I was in the room with the switch in question

Did we adequately communicate to our users?

This event had minimal user-facing impact.

This was on a Friday evening during the scheduled buildout, so no communication was sent to end-users.

Mitigation

What went well that helped minimize the impact of this problem?

I was onsite during configuration of the link.

What can be done to minimize the impact if a problem like this recurs?

Nothing, this is a very rare occurrence.
