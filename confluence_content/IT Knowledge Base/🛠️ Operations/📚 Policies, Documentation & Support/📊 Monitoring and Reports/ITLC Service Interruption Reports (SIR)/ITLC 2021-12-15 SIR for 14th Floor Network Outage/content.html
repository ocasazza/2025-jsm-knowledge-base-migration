Executive summary

Issues with pdx-idf14-r01 switch stack led to clients on that floor not being able to get DHCP leases, eventually leading to downtime requiring a reboot of the entire stack. This also manifested in PDI/UPS being unreachable on the networks and the WAPs being unable to communicate with the controller.


Background

SYSMGR-60275 and SYSMGR-60541 both detail similar situations leading to floor-wide outages.

Incident Description

The PDX 14th Floor IDF Network Switch Stack entered an unstable state after two of the leader members of the stack rebooted themselves.  The root cause of the self-reboot is un-knowable due to our lack of SysLog, but it is most likely due to Control Plane CPU contention from the Stack Link between Switch 1 & 8 flapping continually. Even though the switch stack came back after that reboot, it was not properly forwarding all frames.

Chronology

2:16am PDT, first PagerDuty alerts for pdx-pdu-idf14-a01 and pdx-ups-idf14-d01 being offline.


7:00am PDT logs into the switch stack and discovered the stack has had a failover event and switch 3 is the current leader:



7:30am PDT, gets to his desk and starts investigating. He finds that the two leader members of the stack had rebooted themselves:











He continued to inspect the logs and saw that the link between Switch 1 and 8 had been flapping continually:


8:25am PDT, decided to reboot the stack after changing the priority to the below config:



8:30am PDT, The Stack is back online and looking healthy:


Services began to return to a healthy state and alerts eventually resolved themselves.


8:40am PDT, logs into the PDUs & UPSs to see if there was a power outage that caused these two switches to reboot.  There was not, this was purely a stack-breaking-itself event.


9:06am PDT, issue is resolved.

Corrective Action

initiated a reboot of the entire switch stack. He is also re-configured the switch stack priority to have more deterministically weighted leadership election and ensure that the switch with the fiber link to the MDF was always the leader if it was present.

Analysis

During the stack reboot, went into Libre to put the switch in Maintenance Mode to not generate alerts for the reboot, and he found that the switch had Alerting already disabled, which is why the first alerts of this outage were from the PDU/UPS:



Additionally, this switch stack only has 1 (of 3) links between the 14th floor and the MDF active. does not know why this is the case. At one point in time it looks like all 3 of these fibers were used for improved bandwidth and redundancy, but at some point the other 2 were removed, creating a single point of failure in the network.


Detection

How quickly was the problem detected?

The outage was detected immediately by LibreNMS, though the source and extent took some time to diagnose.

How was the problem detected?

LibreNMS detected an outage for multiple devices at 2:06am.

How can detection be improved?

Detection was immediate.

Response

How quickly did we respond once detected?

LibreNMS alerted PagerDuty of the outage at ~2:16am PDT. The alert was acknowledged by at 3:34am PDT. At 5:51am posted about the PDU being unreachable in SYSMGR.

At 5:57am notified the SYSMGR chat that he'd been paged for pdx-UPS-idf14-a01 and notified Austin, CJ, and Alex. When we realized that it was both the UPS and PDU that were offline, we realized that the outage was more than a single device and needed attention. At this point, work on the issue began in earnest.

Did we adequately communicate to our users?

No communication was sent to end users.

If not, how can this be improved?

Not sure if fully applicable as this outage was resolved before normal office hours in PDX, so this outage shouldn't have effected any users.


Mitigation

What went well that helped minimize the impact of this problem?

Teamwork helped diagnose and resolve the issue quickly.

What can be done to minimize the impact if a problem like this recurs?

Better monitoring, and not having Alerting disabled for network devices would have helped us catch the root cause sooner.  Additionally, we need to replace this flapping stack cable, and we need to get the redundant fiber between 14 and the MDF back in action.

Avoidance

What can we do in the future to avoid a problem like this?

We should probably upgrade the firmware on these switches and maybe refresh the Optics/Cables that could be dying. These cables are very cheap so this should be trivial with a short maintenance window.
