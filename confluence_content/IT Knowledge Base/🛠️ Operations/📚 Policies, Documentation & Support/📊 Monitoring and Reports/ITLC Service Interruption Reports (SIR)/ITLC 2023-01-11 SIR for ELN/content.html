Executive summary

On January 11th our internal users of ELN1 reported that they could not log in. Initially this resembled a few prior instances where this broke as a result of incomplete upgrades but that wasn't the case this time. In this case the D: drive data device that holds the SQL databases and application code was filled to capacity robbing the database and application of space to perform their operations.

The immediate fix for this was to expand the D: disk device from 500GB to 600GB to provide the application and database the space needed to operate.

Background

The ELN1 server is hosted in Amazon web services and consists of a single server comprising both backend database and web application server. The underlying disk storage here is provided by Amazon EC2 with the data drive comprised, initially, of 500GB of type gp2 and the backup drive, again initially, 500GB of type sc1 .

At least twice in the past the ELN vendor Scilligence has unilaterally updated this application and failed to carry-over necessary Okta code into the new version that resulted in internal users being unable to login.

Analysis

There are a couple of proximate causes or contributory causes at play here. The most proximate and obvious being that the disk filled up and app stopped working. Contributing or exacerbating that was that the database maintenance plans included steps to cleanup and delete backup files that weren't doing their jobs. I also found a full database backup (~150GB) from May of '22 in amongst the database files on the D: drive. If this file had been found initially we might have avoided expanding the D: drive or forestalled but not avoided this outage. Some of these causes feedback upon themselves so this might be better represented graphically.

Detection

How quickly was the problem detected?

The time between the disk filling and the application being confirmed available again was approximately 40h of wall clock time.

How was the problem detected?

The problem was detected and reported by users in the 'ELN Service' gchat room.

How can detection be improved?

This forms the likely crux of our response here. If we'd had detection configured to report the system being at or near capacity and been responsive to it we'd likely have been able to avoid an error. Even without active monitoring periodic manual checks on database size or on backup success/failure could have avoided this.

Response

How quickly did we respond once detected?

Response here was delayed by some assumptions that were made when the problem presented about both the nature of the problem and user base it affected. With the problem being reported near the end of business in Portland and the supposition that only internal users were affected I made a conscious personal decision to hold over actions to the morning. With both of those assumptions proved false this decision proved poor.

Did we adequately communicate to our users?

This is unlikely given that the impact of this outage would be felt by our CRO partner organizations. IT has no mechanism to reach this user base and alert them.  When the problem was identified and corrected our ELN business reps took care of reaching out to the CROs to confirm that they were able to interact with the service again.

If not, how can this be improved?

The nature of this service and the business relationships makes this an unlikely and problematic thing to directly improve at least as far as sharing this with the CROs. If we were inclined to invest time in it a mechanism for placing alerts on the WAF (Web Application Firewall) that fronts the ELN might be useful in at least letting the CROs know that the system is down either because of an outage or for maintenance like planned vendor upgrades.

Mitigation

What went well that helped minimize the impact of this problem?

The flexibility afforded by provisioning this service in AWS made expanding the disk both straightforward and relatively fast. So once the problem was identified we could enact the fix quickly.

What can be done to minimize the impact if a problem like this recurs?

Avoidance is the better strategy here but there's a teachable moment here about not jumping to conclusions and making assumptions that could have shortened the outage time here considerably.

Avoidance

What can we do in the future to avoid a problem like this?

Implement actionable monitoring for low disk space conditions on AWS hosted VMs. We'd need to be careful as always that these alerts not get swamped in the sea of incoming email alerts. Recent proposals for segmenting incoming email would probably really help here as the alert under that plan would be more targeted and thus more likely to be addressed promptly.
