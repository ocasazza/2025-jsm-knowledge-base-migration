Executive summary

The SAN office lost internet access. We determined the issue was related to two separate factors, apparently happening at the same time. First, the DMZ switch that provides primary internet access was unplugged. Second, desk installers created two loops causing catastrophic broadcast storms.

Background

Loops are very bad. The DMZ should remain plugged in at all times.

Incident Description

Two separate issues occurred here:

First, the DMZ switch became unplugged.

Second, two loops were created by desk installers.

Chronology

It's currently unknown which happened first, the DMZ switch going down, or the loops being created.

Corrective Action

We unplugged the looped ports, and plugged the DMZ switch back in.

Analysis

Somehow, locking connectors were not enough to prevent the DMZ switch from being unplugged. I'm unsure of how we could improve this further.

Mitigation

What went well that helped minimize the impact of this problem?

STP is not used, our ruckus MCT cluster stayed usable during a 100+ gigabit flood. Link status reflected the state well, and the fortigates attempted to failover the ISP.

What can be done to minimize the impact if a problem like this recurs?

Validation of what is being plugged into where, and more care in the datacenter.

Potentially better tracking of DC activities, like a sign in/out clipboard by the door.

Investigation into strategies for detection of loops / broadcast floods.

Avoidance

What can we do in the future to avoid a problem like this?

Better loop detection support using the tools available on the ruckus ICX platform.

Detection of broadcast storms via libre port broadcast % alerts.
